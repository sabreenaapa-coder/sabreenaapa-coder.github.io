Why it exists

The adhan is in our pockets — but not in our walls.

Be honest about the phone app for a moment.

When Asr comes in, your phone buzzes — face-down, on silent, in another room. Does your mother in the next room hear it? No. Do the children look up? No. It reminds one person, once, if that person happens to be looking.

There was a time when the call to prayer marked the rhythm of the whole day, for the whole household. Children grew up hearing it. Families paused together. Nobody had to remember, because the house itself remembered.

CastAdhan puts the call back in the house. Every prayer, in every room, on the speakers you already own — whether anyone remembers or not. You set it up once, then never touch it again.

The cost, in perspective

CastAdhan is a one-off purchase — no subscription, no monthly fee, and it updates itself for life. At £85, if it serves your home for just five years, that works out around 33p a week for the adhan at every prayer, in every room. Most families spend more than that on things they can't remember by Friday.

Who this is really for

The busy parentwho wants the children to grow up with the adhan as a normal, daily sound.
The grandparentswho shouldn't need to master an app for their home to remember Allah.
The convert, or the family far from a mosquefor whom the nearest adhan is a long drive away.
The household that means to pray on timeand keeps finding out the window closed twenty minutes ago.

One last thing

Tomorrow morning, Fajr will come in whether the house hears it or not. Another Friday will pass, another Surah al‑Kahf unread, another week where the adhan stayed in a pocket.

It doesn't have to.
